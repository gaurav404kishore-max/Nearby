Product case study
Google shows you where ATMs are. Not what they do.
NearBy was built to solve a real frustration — walking to an ATM only to find it doesn't support cash deposit. Or driving to a petrol pump that's closed. Location without capability is incomplete information.
The problem
Utility search is broken for high-urgency, time-sensitive needs. Google Maps tells you where an ATM is. It doesn't tell you whether it accepts deposits, whether it has cash, or whether it's actually functional. You only find out when you're standing in front of it.
The same gap exists for petrol pumps (what fuel type? open right now?) and pharmacies (24 hours? specific medicines?). These aren't discovery needs — they're utility needs. The failure cost is a wasted trip.
Who it's for
Urban India, 25–45, smartphone-first. Specifically: someone who needs to deposit cash before a bank deadline. Someone running low on fuel late at night in an unfamiliar area. Someone looking for a 24-hour pharmacy at 11pm. Common thread: high intent, time-sensitive, zero tolerance for wrong results.
Key product decisions
Why no ratings or reviews?
Every feature that adds noise to a utility app is a failure of product thinking. You don't rate an ATM. You use it or you don't. Reviews would increase cognitive load at the exact moment users need speed.
Why "Open now" is always on by default?
Nobody searches for a closed ATM intentionally. Defaulting to open results removes one decision from an already urgent moment. You can always turn it off — but most users never need to.
Why three categories and not more?
ATM, petrol, pharmacy represent the three highest-urgency utility searches in urban India. Adding more categories dilutes the focus. A product that does three things perfectly beats one that does ten things adequately.
Why list-first, not map-first?
When you need a specific capability, distance ranking matters more than spatial layout. The list answers "which one should I go to" faster than a map. The map answers "where exactly is it" — which is step two, not step one.
What we measure
Navigate taps per session — the north star. If someone taps navigate, the app did its job. That's the job to be done, done.
Time to first result — how quickly does a user see usable results from landing. Under 5 seconds is the target.
Filter usage — which capability filters are used most tells us what the real unmet needs are, and what data gaps to close first.
Return rate — high return = genuine utility, not curiosity. A useful utility app becomes habitual.
On the data
Location data is sourced from OpenStreetMap via the Overpass API — free and open source. Capability data (deposit, cardless, cash level) is illustrative. In a production build, this would connect to bank-published APIs or a community-verified data layer. The product insight is the design — the data pipeline is an integration problem, not a product problem.
